# Astrological & Planetary Cycles in Financial Markets

## Overview

The use of celestial cycles for market timing is one of the oldest and most controversial approaches in financial analysis. Despite mainstream academia's dismissal, a persistent community of practitioners — some with impressive track records — has applied planetary cycles, lunar phases, solar activity, and astrological aspects to market forecasting. This document surveys the methods, the practitioners, the academic evidence (both for and against), and the specific claimed mechanisms.

The key question isn't "is this astrology?" — it's **"do celestial cycles correlate with market behavior, and if so, through what mechanism?"** The answers are more nuanced than either side typically acknowledges.

## 1. Lunar Cycles and Market Returns

### The Academic Evidence: Dichev & Janes (2003)

The most rigorous academic study on lunar effects in financial markets is **"Lunar Cycle Effects in Stock Returns"** by Ilia D. Dichev (University of Michigan) and Troy D. Janes (San Francisco State University), published in the *Journal of Private Equity* (2003), with earlier working paper versions circulating from 2001 and a related paper in the *Journal of Finance* (2002).

#### Study Design
- **Data**: Daily returns from **48 stock market indices across 25 countries**
- **Time period**: 1973–2001 (25+ years)
- **Method**: Compared average daily log returns during **waxing phases** (new moon → full moon, ~14.75 days) vs. **waning phases** (full moon → new moon)
- **Lunar data**: Precise astronomical data from the U.S. Naval Observatory
- **Controls**: Adjusted for day-of-week effects, January effect, volatility, and trading volume

#### Key Results

| Phase | Avg. Daily Return | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| **Waxing** (New → Full Moon) | +0.076% | Highly significant (t-stat > 3 across indices) |
| **Waning** (Full → New Moon) | −0.017% | Often insignificant or negative |
| **Difference** | +0.093% | p < 0.01 for most indices |

- **U.S. S&P 500 specifically**: Waxing returns +0.049% vs. waning −0.015% (t-stat 1.94, marginally significant)
- **Effect was strongest in emerging markets**
- Effect held in **37 of 48 indices** examined
- **Not explained** by trading volume, volatility, or calendar anomalies
- **Annualized**: The lunar effect implies a ~10–20% return boost for perfectly timing trades around moon phases
- **Robust across subsamples**: Effect persists pre- and post-1987 crash

#### Impact & Replication
- Over **1,500 citations** on Google Scholar
- **Yuan et al. (2006)** replicated findings in Chinese markets
- **Herbst (2007)** found similar effects in commodity markets
- **Criticism**: Transaction costs would likely erode most of the theoretical edge; the effect is statistically significant but economically small for individual trades

#### Proposed Mechanism
The authors suggest **lunar influence on human mood/psychology** as the channel:
- Psychological research documents mood variations correlated with lunar phases
- More optimistic mood during waxing phase → more buying → higher returns
- More pessimistic mood during waning phase → more selling → lower returns
- This is a **behavioral finance** explanation, not a mystical one

### Practical Trading Applications
Some traders use lunar cycles as a **filter** rather than a standalone signal:
- Enter long positions preferentially during new moon periods
- Take profits or reduce exposure during full moon periods
- Use moon phases as a tiebreaker when other signals are ambiguous

## 2. Mercury Retrograde and Markets

### What Is Mercury Retrograde?
Mercury retrograde is an optical illusion where Mercury appears to move backward in its orbit as viewed from Earth, occurring **3–4 times per year for approximately 3 weeks each time**. In astrology, it's associated with communication breakdowns, technology failures, and confusion.

### Market Claims
Financial astrologers claim Mercury retrograde periods correlate with:
- Increased market volatility and confusion
- Technology-related disruptions (flash crashes, trading system errors)
- Poor decision-making leading to reversals
- "False signals" in technical analysis

### The Evidence

#### Statistical Studies
There is **no peer-reviewed academic study** from a major journal that conclusively demonstrates a Mercury retrograde effect on financial markets. The existing evidence is primarily:

1. **Anecdotal correlation tracking** by financial astrologers who maintain that markets tend to be more volatile or directionless during retrograde periods
2. **Confirmation bias** is the most commonly cited counter-explanation: "When Mercury goes retrograde and the market drops, believers remember it. When Mercury goes retrograde and the market rises, they forget it or explain it away"
3. Some proprietary research (not peer-reviewed) claims to find **lower average returns** during retrograde periods, but sample sizes are small and methodology is often questionable

#### Notable Retrograde-Market Coincidences (Cited by Practitioners)
- Mercury was retrograde during several notable market disruptions, though given that Mercury is retrograde ~19% of the time, some overlap with market events is statistically expected
- Practitioners like the **MMA Cycles** (Merriman Market Analyst) group track Mercury retrograde as one factor among many in their timing models

#### Honest Assessment
Mercury retrograde's effect on markets is the **weakest claim** in financial astrology. Even most financial astrologers view it as a minor factor, not a primary timing tool. The ~19% base rate of retrograde periods means any pattern is easily confused with noise.

## 3. Saturn-Jupiter Conjunctions and Economic Cycles

### The 20-Year Cycle
Jupiter and Saturn conjoin approximately every **19.86 years** (~20 years). This is one of the oldest observed planetary cycles, studied since Babylonian times.

#### Historical Conjunction Timeline

| Date | Sign | Notable Economic Events |
|------|------|------------------------|
| 1901 | Capricorn | Start of early 20th century industrialization boom |
| 1921 | Virgo | Post-WWI recession → start of Roaring Twenties |
| 1940–41 | Taurus | WWII economic mobilization; end of Great Depression |
| 1961 | Capricorn | Kennedy-era expansion; beginning of 1960s bull market |
| 1980–81 | Libra | Volcker recession; end of 1970s stagflation; new bull cycle |
| 2000 | Taurus | Dot-com bust; start of new millennium downturn |
| 2020 | Aquarius | COVID crash & recovery; massive fiscal/monetary intervention |

#### The Claimed Pattern
Financial astrologers observe that Jupiter-Saturn conjunctions tend to coincide with **major economic inflection points** — the end of one economic era and the beginning of another.

- **Jupiter** represents expansion, growth, optimism, and risk-taking
- **Saturn** represents contraction, limitation, discipline, and restructuring
- Their conjunction is interpreted as a **reset point** where the old cycle of expansion meets the new cycle's consolidation
- The years around the conjunction often feature significant economic policy changes, market regime shifts, or geopolitical realignments

#### Connection to Kondratieff Waves
Some researchers have noted that **three Jupiter-Saturn conjunctions** (~60 years) roughly correspond to one **Kondratieff long wave** (the ~50–60 year economic supercycle identified by Russian economist Nikolai Kondratieff). This is suggestive but not causally established:
- Kondratieff waves: ~50–60 years (based on commodity prices, interest rates, innovation cycles)
- 3 × Jupiter-Saturn cycles: ~59.6 years
- The overlap may be coincidental, or both may reflect an underlying ~60-year rhythm in human economic activity

#### Critical Assessment
The 20-year cycle has remarkably consistent alignment with economic turning points. However:
- With only ~6 conjunctions in the modern financial era (since 1900), the sample size is too small for statistical significance
- The "events" that coincide with conjunctions are selected retroactively
- 20-year intervals will inevitably overlap with *some* major event given the frequency of economic disruptions
- The mechanism (how planetary gravity could affect economic behavior) remains entirely speculative

## 4. Solar Cycles (11-Year Sunspot Cycle)

### Background
The Sun follows an approximately **11-year cycle** of magnetic activity, measured by sunspot counts. This cycle is well-documented in physics and has measurable effects on Earth's magnetosphere, climate, and communications.

### Historical Origins: The Jevons Hypothesis
**William Stanley Jevons** (1835–1882), a prominent British economist, proposed in the 1870s that sunspot cycles influence agricultural output through climate effects, which in turn drive economic cycles. While his specific agricultural mechanism has been largely dismissed, the broader question of solar-economic correlations has persisted.

### Academic Research

#### Dow Jones / Sunspot Correlation Study
A study examining the **Dow Jones Industrial Average from 1896 to 2021** (published in the Georgian journal *Geosciences*) found:
- Correlation with 11-year periodicity **exceeded 0.5** during the periods **1906–1936** and **1964–2000**
- Reached **95% statistical confidence** during **1910–1930** and **1990–1994**
- High coherence between **cosmic ray data** and the Dow Jones during **1964–2000**
- The correlation appears **episodic** — strong in some periods, weak or absent in others

#### Notable Coincidences
- The **1929 stock market crash** occurred during a **solar maximum** (peak sunspot activity)
- **Solar Cycle 19** (peak 1958) coincided with strong market performance
- **Solar Cycle 24** (2008–2019, weakest in 100 years) coincided with the longest bull market in history (2009–2020) — which contradicts a simple "more sunspots = better markets" model

#### Proposed Mechanisms
1. **Geomagnetic storms** → affect human psychology/mood → affect risk appetite
   - **Krivelyova & Robotti (2003)** at the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta found that geomagnetic storms (caused by solar activity) are associated with **lower stock returns** in the following days
   - The proposed channel: geomagnetic disturbances affect melatonin production and mood
2. **Climate effects** → affect agricultural output → affect commodity prices → affect broader economy (Jevons updated)
3. **Cosmic ray modulation** → during solar minimum, more cosmic rays reach Earth → potential effects on cloud formation, climate, and by extension economic activity

#### Assessment
The solar cycle has the **strongest scientific basis** of any celestial market correlation because:
- The 11-year cycle is a proven physical phenomenon
- Geomagnetic effects on biology are documented
- The correlation, while episodic, has been found in multiple independent studies
- The proposed mechanisms (geomagnetic → mood → risk appetite) are at least plausible

However, the relationship is **not consistent enough** to be a reliable trading tool. Solar activity is one of "many simultaneous influences" on markets.

## 5. The Bradley Siderograph

### Origins
Developed by **Donald A. Bradley** in **1948**, published in his booklet *"Stock Market Prediction: The Planetary Barometer and How to Use It."* It was one of the first attempts to create a **quantitative, computable index** from planetary positions.

### How It Works

#### The Formula
The Siderograph is a composite index calculated from the **geocentric ecliptic longitudes** of six celestial bodies: Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn.

Each planet receives a **weight**:

| Planet | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Moon | 43.5 |
| Venus | 15.0 |
| Mars | 12.5 |
| Mercury | 12.0 |
| Jupiter | 9.0 |
| Saturn | 8.0 |
| **Total** | **100.0** |

For each planet, a **harmonic function** is applied to its ecliptic longitude, producing a sinusoidal wave that peaks at "power zones" (specific degree positions). The weighted sum of all planetary harmonics produces the Siderograph value for any given date.

#### Simplified Mathematical Expression
```
S(t) = 43.5·sin(3·λ_Moon) + 15.0·sin(4·(λ_Venus - 30°)) + 12.5·sin(2·λ_Mars) 
     + 12.0·sin(4·λ_Mercury) + 9.0·sin(λ_Jupiter) + 8.0·sin(2·λ_Saturn)
```

Where λ represents the geocentric ecliptic longitude of each planet at time t.

#### Interpretation
- The Siderograph produces an oscillating line
- **Turning points** in the Siderograph (peaks and troughs) are predicted to coincide with turning points in the stock market
- **The direction doesn't matter** — a Siderograph peak could coincide with either a market peak OR a market trough
- Only the **timing of turns** is predicted, not the direction

### Historical Performance
- Bradley's original 1948 work claimed correlation with Dow Jones turning points
- The model has had **notable hits** (e.g., some practitioners noted a 2020 COVID low aligning with a Siderograph trough around March 23, 2020)
- However, it has also had many **misses**
- No peer-reviewed study has confirmed the Siderograph's predictive power exceeds chance

### Modern Availability
- Computed freely using **JPL/NASA ephemeris data**
- Available as **TradingView scripts** and **Python libraries** on GitHub
- Used primarily by niche financial astrology practitioners, not mainstream traders

## 6. Modern Practitioners

### Arch Crawford (1941–2019)
**Crawford Perspectives** newsletter

- One of the most prominent financial astrologers of the late 20th century
- Published **Crawford Perspectives**, a market timing newsletter, from the 1970s until his death
- **Ranked #1 market timer** by *Timer Digest* multiple times (a publication that independently tracks the performance of market timing newsletters)
- His most famous call: **predicted the 1987 crash** in advance based on a rare alignment of planets
  - Specifically cited the August 1987 Harmonic Convergence (a New Age event based on Mayan calendar alignment) and a subsequent lunar eclipse as danger signals
  - Issued a sell signal before Black Monday (October 19, 1987)
- **Methodology**: Combined heliocentric astrology, geocentric aspects, eclipse cycles, and technical analysis
- Focused primarily on **outer planet aspects** (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Pluto) for major market cycles
- Used **geocentric aspects** (Mercury, Venus, Mars) for shorter-term timing

#### Track Record Assessment
Crawford's track record was genuinely impressive by newsletter standards — Timer Digest's rankings are based on actual published calls, not retroactive claims. However:
- No newsletter timer beats the market consistently over very long periods
- His biggest misses are less publicized than his biggest hits
- His success may partially reflect that astrological timing, being uncorrelated with traditional indicators, occasionally captures turns that conventional analysis misses

### Ray Merriman — Merriman Market Analyst (MMA Cycles)
**Website**: mmacycles.com

- **President of Merriman Market Analysts**, founded in the 1980s
- Arguably the most **methodologically rigorous** modern financial astrologer
- **Published works** include:
  - *"The Ultimate Book on Stock Market Timing"* (multi-volume series)
  - *"The Sun, the Moon, and the Silver Market"*
  - *"Basic Principles of Geocosmic Studies for Financial Market Timing"*
  - *"Evolutionary Astrology"*
- **Approach**: Blends planetary cycles with traditional technical analysis and cycle theory
  - Identifies planetary aspects as **primary timing factors**
  - Uses technical analysis (moving averages, chart patterns) for **confirmation and direction**
  - Does NOT use astrology alone — always combines with market structure analysis
- Conducts **statistical research** on planetary cycle correlations
  - Maintains databases of planetary aspects and market turning points
  - Publishes correlation studies (though not in peer-reviewed academic journals)
- Provides **weekly forecasts** covering stocks, gold, silver, Bitcoin, and currencies
- Hosts annual **Cycles Conference** bringing together cycle analysts, technical analysts, and financial astrologers

#### MMA Cycles Methodology
Merriman uses a multi-layer approach:
1. **Long-term cycles**: Saturn-Uranus (45-year), Saturn-Neptune (36-year), Jupiter-Saturn (20-year)
2. **Intermediate cycles**: Jupiter aspects (12-year), Saturn aspects (14.5-year opposition)
3. **Short-term cycles**: Mars-Jupiter, Venus-Mars, Mercury retrograde
4. **Micro-timing**: Lunar phases, planetary ingresses

### Other Notable Practitioners

- **Bill Meridian** — author of *"Planetary Stock Trading"*, applied statistical methods to astrological hypotheses
- **Christeen Skinner** — UK-based financial astrologer, author of *"The Financial Universe"*

## 7. Academic & Statistical Evidence: The Balance Sheet

### Evidence FOR Celestial Market Correlations

| Study/Finding | Type | Result |
|---------------|------|--------|
| **Dichev & Janes (2003)** — Lunar cycle study | Peer-reviewed (Journal of Finance) | Statistically significant difference in returns between waxing/waning moon phases across 48 global indices |
| **Krivelyova & Robotti (2003)** — Geomagnetic storms | Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta working paper | Geomagnetic storms associated with lower stock returns in following days |
| **Georgian solar cycle study (2021)** | Academic journal | Significant correlation between 11-year sunspot cycle and DJIA in certain periods (1906–1936, 1964–2000) |
| **Yuan et al. (2006)** — Lunar cycle replication | Academic paper | Confirmed Dichev-Janes findings in Chinese markets |
| **Arch Crawford's Timer Digest rankings** | Industry performance tracking | Ranked #1 market timer multiple times using astrology-based methods |
| **Herbst (2007)** — Lunar effects on commodities | Academic paper | Found lunar cycle effects in commodity markets |

### Evidence AGAINST Celestial Market Correlations

| Argument | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **No causal mechanism** | There is no established physical mechanism by which planetary positions (other than Sun and Moon) could influence human behavior or markets |
| **Confirmation bias** | Practitioners remember hits and forget misses; patterns are found retroactively |
| **Data mining / p-hacking** | With thousands of possible planetary aspects and market events, some correlations will appear by chance |
| **Small effect sizes** | Even statistically significant findings (lunar cycle) are too small to profit from after transaction costs |
| **Episodic correlations** | The solar cycle correlation appears and disappears across different time periods |
| **No consistent replication** | Mercury retrograde effects have not been demonstrated in any rigorous study |
| **Efficient Market Hypothesis** | If astrology worked, arbitrageurs would exploit it until the edge disappeared |
| **Base rate fallacy** | Planetary aspects occur frequently enough that some will inevitably coincide with market turns |

### The Nuanced View

The evidence suggests a **gradient of credibility**:

1. **Most credible**: Solar cycle effects (established physical phenomenon with documented biological effects; episodic but real correlations found)
2. **Moderately credible**: Lunar cycle effects (peer-reviewed evidence from a top finance journal; small but statistically significant; plausible mood-based mechanism)
3. **Weakly credible**: Major planetary conjunctions as economic cycle markers (interesting historical alignment but sample size too small; no mechanism)
4. **Least credible**: Mercury retrograde, specific planetary aspects, the Siderograph (no peer-reviewed evidence; strong confirmation bias risks; no mechanism)

## 8. How Practitioners Actually Use These Tools

### The Layered Approach
Serious financial astrologers (as opposed to pop astrology columnists) typically:

1. **Never use astrology alone** — it's always combined with technical and/or fundamental analysis
2. **Use planetary cycles for TIMING, not direction** — the cycle identifies WHEN a turn might occur; traditional analysis identifies which direction
3. **Treat cycles as probabilities, not certainties** — a planetary aspect creates a "time window" of heightened reversal probability, not a guaranteed turning point
4. **Focus on confluence** — the more independent factors (planetary aspects, technical levels, Fibonacci targets, seasonal patterns) that agree on a date/price, the higher the confidence
5. **Maintain statistical databases** — tracking which aspects have historically correlated with what market behavior

### Typical Workflow
1. Check **long-term planetary cycle** position (where are we in the Saturn-Uranus, Jupiter-Saturn, etc. cycles?)
2. Identify **upcoming medium-term aspects** (eclipses, major planetary aspects in next 1–3 months)
3. Check **short-term factors** (lunar phase, Mercury retrograde, planetary ingresses)
4. Overlay this timing framework on **technical analysis** (chart patterns, support/resistance, momentum indicators)
5. Only take trades where **multiple independent factors align**

## Summary

Celestial cycle trading is neither pure mysticism nor established science. It occupies a **liminal zone** where:

- A small number of effects (lunar returns, geomagnetic storm effects) have **genuine peer-reviewed support** from legitimate academic journals
- Some practitioners (Crawford, Merriman) have achieved **independently verified** track records that outperform many conventional approaches
- The **mechanisms** are largely unknown — the most plausible involve solar/lunar effects on human biology and mood, not gravitational influences from distant planets
- The **effect sizes** are generally small — too small to build a standalone trading system, but potentially valuable as one factor among many
- The field is plagued by **confirmation bias, data mining, and unfalsifiable claims** that make it difficult to separate signal from noise

The most intellectually honest approach: take the peer-reviewed evidence seriously (lunar cycles, geomagnetic storms), treat the practitioner-reported patterns with respectful skepticism (planetary conjunctions, Bradley Siderograph), and dismiss the unsupported claims (Mercury retrograde as a market driver). Use celestial cycles as **one input in a multi-factor timing framework**, never as a standalone system.
